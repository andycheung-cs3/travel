Forbidden City

The Forbidden City was commissioned by Emperor Yongle of the Ming dynasty and constructed between 1406 and 1420. Designed by architect Kuai Xiang, it became the residence of 24 emperors from the Ming and Qing dynasties. For centuries, it functioned as the political, ceremonial, and administrative center of imperial China, where emperors ruled and conducted state affairs. Its name, “Forbidden City,” reflects the restricted access ordinary citizens had—only the emperor, his family, and select officials could enter its gates.

Covering 72 hectares with nearly 1,000 buildings, the Forbidden City is the largest palace complex in the world. Its layout follows strict principles of traditional Chinese architecture, emphasizing symmetry and hierarchy. Key structures include the Hall of Supreme Harmony, used for grand ceremonies, and the Palace of Heavenly Purity, the emperor’s residence. The complex embodies Confucian ideals of order and authority, while its artistry—such as intricate carvings, golden roofs, and red walls—showcases the pinnacle of Chinese craftsmanship. In 1987, UNESCO recognized it as a World Heritage Site for its outstanding cultural value.

Since 1925, the Forbidden City has been open to the public as the Palace Museum, attracting millions of visitors annually (over 16 million in recent years). It houses vast collections of imperial treasures, artworks, and historical artifacts, making it a cornerstone of Chinese cultural preservation. Beyond tourism, it serves as a powerful symbol of national identity, linking modern China to its imperial past. Preservation efforts continue to protect its ancient structures against environmental and human impact, ensuring that future generations can experience this monumental site.
