Paris history

The origins of Paris date to around 250 BC, when the Celtic Parisii tribe settled along the banks of the Seine. They built fortified structures known as oppida, laying the groundwork for the city’s strategic importance. When the Romans conquered the region in the 1st century BC, they renamed the settlement Lutetia, turning it into a thriving provincial town with roads, aqueducts, and amphitheaters. Roman influence established Paris as a center of trade and governance, and remnants of this era, such as the Arènes de Lutèce, still survive today.

By the Middle Ages, Paris had become the capital of the Kingdom of France, with the construction of landmarks like Notre-Dame Cathedral (begun in 1163) symbolizing its growing religious and cultural power. The city flourished as a hub of learning, with the University of Paris gaining renown across Europe. However, Paris also endured hardships, including the Hundred Years’ War and outbreaks of plague. In the late 18th century, Paris became the epicenter of the French Revolution (1789–1799), where events like the storming of the Bastille reshaped France’s political and social order.

The 19th century saw Paris undergo dramatic modernization under Baron Haussmann, who redesigned the city with wide boulevards, parks, and uniform architecture, giving Paris much of its current appearance. During the Belle Époque (1871–1914), Paris emerged as a global capital of art, fashion, and innovation, hosting the Exposition Universelle and unveiling the Eiffel Tower in 1889. The city endured occupation during World War II, but postwar Paris regained its cultural leadership, becoming a symbol of intellectual life, fashion, and diplomacy. Today, Paris remains a vibrant blend of its ancient heritage and modern dynamism, embodying centuries of history in its streets and monuments.
