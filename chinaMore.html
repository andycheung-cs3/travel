China history

China’s earliest recorded dynasty, the Xia (c. 2070–1600 BCE), is often considered semi-legendary, but it set the stage for the Shang Dynasty (c. 1600–1046 BCE), which introduced bronze technology, writing systems, and complex social structures. The Zhou Dynasty (c. 1046–256 BCE) followed, establishing the concept of the Mandate of Heaven, which justified rulers’ authority as divinely sanctioned. During the Zhou era, China experienced the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods, times of philosophical flourishing with Confucius, Laozi, and other thinkers shaping Chinese culture and governance.

In 221 BCE, the Qin Dynasty unified China under Emperor Qin Shi Huang, standardizing laws, currency, and even the written script. The subsequent Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE) expanded trade through the Silk Road, connecting China to Central Asia and Europe. Later dynasties such as the Tang (618–907 CE) and Song (960–1279 CE) ushered in golden ages of art, literature, and technological innovation, including printing and gunpowder. The Mongol-led Yuan Dynasty (1271–1368 CE) and the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644 CE) further shaped China’s global influence, with the Ming overseeing maritime expeditions led by Admiral Zheng He.

The Qing Dynasty (1644–1911 CE) was China’s last imperial dynasty, marked by territorial expansion but also internal strife and foreign pressures. The fall of the Qing led to the Republic of China (1912), which struggled with warlordism and foreign invasions, particularly during World War II. In 1949, the People’s Republic of China was established under Mao Zedong, ushering in communist rule. Since the late 20th century, China has undergone rapid economic growth and modernization, becoming a global superpower while maintaining deep ties to its historical traditions.
